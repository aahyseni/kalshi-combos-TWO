RISK ENGINE BRIEFING — combomaker (a Kalshi combo/parlay market maker)
Prepared 2026-07-12 for external review. Self-contained: assumes no prior context.

PURPOSE OF THIS DOCUMENT
------------------------
This describes the RISK ENGINE we are building for an automated market-making
bot, plus enough background about the bot and its market to make the risk design
legible. It is written to be handed to a second AI/analyst for a critical
second opinion. Please be skeptical: point out flaws, missing failure modes,
wrong assumptions, and better designs. Section 10 lists the specific questions
we most want challenged.


================================================================================
1. WHAT THE PROJECT IS (one paragraph)
================================================================================
"combomaker" is an automated market maker for COMBO markets (parlays / same-game
parlays / cross-game bundles) on Kalshi, a regulated US prediction-market
exchange. A combo is a single binary contract whose YES pays $1 if EVERY leg of
the bundle comes true, and whose NO pays $1 if ANY leg fails. Combos arrive as
RFQs (requests-for-quote): a customer proposes a bundle of legs and asks makers
to quote it. Our bot prices the combo and posts a quote. It is currently in
observe/paper mode; going live is gated behind explicit config + a configured
risk engine (the subject of this doc). Stack: Python 3.12, async, SQLite,
hand-rolled Kalshi REST/WS client. Money is handled as integer "centi-cents"
(1 cent = 100 units; $1 = 10,000 units) — binary floats for money are banned.


================================================================================
2. THE MARKET MECHANICS (critical to get right)
================================================================================
2.1 Binary contracts. A Kalshi binary has a YES and a NO side; YES price + NO
price = $1. If you BUY a NO contract at price p_no, you pay p_no upfront and
receive $1 if the event resolves NO (else $0). Your max loss is exactly p_no
(what you paid) — bought binaries are never margin-called.

2.2 We are a PARLAY SELLER, SELL-ONLY. When we quote a combo we only ever take
the NO side of the parlay: we quote a NO bid (yes_bid forced to 0). Economically
we are "selling the parlay" to the customer who wants YES. So:
  - We hold NO. If the parlay MISSES (any leg fails -> combo settles NO), we get
    $1/contract. If the parlay HITS (all legs true -> settles YES), our NO is
    worthless and we lose what we paid.
  - Parlays usually miss. Empirically ~75% of the combos we'd hold settle NO
    (we win), ~25% settle YES (we pay out).
  - GROUND TRUTH we verified live: a demo combo, 1.00 NO contract, cost $0.50,
    settled NO, paid $1.00 (realized +$0.50). This anchors all our accounting.

2.3 Why sell-only. We measured (5.7M-RFQ backtest) that taking the YES side of
parlay flow is structurally adversely selected (customers who buy parlays lose,
but the maker who accepts the YES side inherits toxic flow). Selling the parlay
(NO) is the +EV side. yes_bid=0 guarantees single-sided exposure.

2.4 Pricing (context; not the subject here but needed). Leg probabilities
("marginals") come LIVE from Kalshi's individual leg order books. The only thing
we model is the JOINT/correlation layer: how legs co-move (a Gaussian copula
with correlation values measured from historical sports data, plus structural
scoreline models, plus exact arithmetic for logically-related legs). Our "fair
value" of a combo = our estimate of P(all legs true). We quote fair + a markup.

2.5 The clearing price. When a combo trades, it trades at a "clearing" price =
the winning maker's fair value PLUS their markup. So clearing is NOT truth; it
is fair+markup. The only unbiased ruler for whether our fair is right is actual
SETTLEMENT (did the parlay hit or miss). We have measured our fair beats the
clearing on settlement calibration (we are closer to realized outcomes than the
market's clearing price is).

3. WHERE THE PROJECT STANDS TODAY
================================================================================
- PRICING is mature: two live sports (World Cup soccer + MLB baseball), every
  correlation pair measured or exact (no hand-guessed priors), adversarially
  audited, backtest-gated. MLB prices to ~0.34 cents median error vs the market;
  soccer beats the market on settlement calibration. Only MLB + World Cup are
  enabled (a hard allowlist blocks all other sports/crypto at intake).
- EXECUTION plumbing exists: RFQ intake, quote lifecycle (quote -> accept ->
  last-look -> confirm/execute), a "pregame-only" gate (never quote a combo any
  of whose legs is already in-play).
- RISK ENGINE is ~80% built but had gaps; we are now hardening it to run a live
  book autonomously. THIS is what the rest of the doc covers.
- Bankroll to START: $2,000 (deliberately small; scale later).


================================================================================
4. THE RISK ENGINE — PHILOSOPHY
================================================================================
The edge is execution discipline, not just pricing. A correctly-priced book can
still blow up from: over-concentration (many combos on one game/leg all breaking
together), mass-acceptance (many resting quotes accepted at once), a bad
correlated week, or a single huge combo. The risk engine must let the bot run
100% autonomously and know, at every instant: its balance, its open legs, its
per-game exposure, its losses, and its headroom against every limit.

Key structural fact discovered empirically: THE TRUE RISK UNIT IS THE GAME, NOT
THE COMBO. Combos share legs and share games; combos on the same match are
highly correlated (they can all break together on one result). So exposure must
be aggregated per GAME, and the scariest scenario is one game resolving entirely
against us.


================================================================================
5. THE POSITION BOOK MODEL (built)
================================================================================
A hierarchical live view: GAME -> LEG -> COMBO. For every position we hold or
could take, we track:
  - Per LEG: net exposure, and P(this leg hits) from our pricing marginals.
  - Per GAME (the aggregation key): worst-case loss if the whole game breaks
    against us (the correlated cluster), and gross payout exposure.
  - Per COMBO: its max loss and its gross payout.
  - Directional bias: are we piled up on the same outcome across many games?

TWO MONEY AXES, tracked separately and NEVER summed (this distinction is
load-bearing):
  (a) MAX-LOSS axis = what we can actually LOSE. For our NO position = contracts
      x (the NO price we paid). On the demo: 1 ct x $0.50 = $0.50. This is the
      true downside — it is what the loss caps limit.
  (b) PAYOUT-OBLIGATION / gross axis = contracts x $1 (the gross settlement
      size). On the demo: $1.00. This is the "how much is locked up / gross
      exposure" number. For longshot combos (NO price near $1) the two axes
      nearly coincide; for near-even combos they diverge.

IMPLEMENTATION NOTE / a correction we made: an early design claimed our max-loss
field was "6-11x too loose" (it assumed the field stored a small YES-premium).
On inspection it already stored the NO price we paid, i.e. the TRUE max loss,
verified against the demo settlement. So the loss accounting was already correct;
what was missing was the separate gross-payout axis (now added) and per-GAME
aggregation (see next).


================================================================================
6. GAME-KEY AGGREGATION (built)
================================================================================
Problem found: exposure was aggregated by Kalshi "event_ticker", but each market
FAMILY of one match (the moneyline market, the totals market, the spread market,
the player-prop markets) is a DIFFERENT event_ticker. So one match's exposure
was silently split across several buckets, and the per-game correlated risk was
invisible. This is the direct cause of a concentration finding (a few games /
combos dominated volume with correlated ~$1M swings) going unseen.

Fix (built): a single canonical GAME KEY = the game-code embedded in the ticker
(e.g. "26JUL09FRAMAR" for a France-Morocco match on Jul 9), the same key the
correlation model already uses. Now every market family of one match aggregates
into ONE game cluster. Verified: moneyline + totals legs of one match fold into
one bucket; two different matches stay separate.


================================================================================
7. LIVE BALANCE TRACKER (built)
================================================================================
The bot must always know its money. A BalanceTracker:
  - Polls the exchange for the live account balance -> the authoritative
    "bankroll" figure. If the poll is stale (older than a threshold), it
    FAILS CLOSED (treats bankroll as unknown -> caps bind conservatively).
  - Runs an internal realized-P&L ledger: on each settlement, NO-miss (we win)
    CREDITS, YES-hit (we lose) DEBITS. This gives a running realized P&L and
    cumulative-loss number queryable at any instant.
  - Design decision (flagged): the live exchange poll is AUTHORITATIVE for the
    bankroll; the internal ledger is a separate cross-check, never summed into
    the bankroll (that would drift from the exchange and lose the fail-closed
    guarantee).

8. THE CAPS / LIMITS / KILL-SWITCH (designed; values sized for $2,000)
================================================================================
All caps expressed as % of bankroll so they scale. Two independent derivations
(risk-of-ruin/Kelly theory + an empirical game-clustered simulation over 17,351
real settled combos across 59 games) were reconciled to these conservative START
values for a $2,000 bankroll:

  Cap                         Start   $ at $2k   Limits
  --------------------------  ------  ---------  ---------------------------------
  %-of-GAME (correlated loss) 8%      $160       max loss if one game breaks fully
  per-COMBO max payout        1%      $20        single combo's max loss (slice whales)
  one-directional / theme     10%     $200       net exposure to one outcome across games
  absolute-$ backstop         3x      $6,000     gross committed payout, whole book
  daily-loss halt (soft)      6%      $120       realized+unrealized in a day -> stop
  peak-drawdown halt          10%     $200       give-back from intraday peak
  hard-trip kill (human clear)12%     $240       KILL file, survives restart
  fill-velocity               5%/2s soft, 10%/2s hard, 8 fills/2s (mass-acceptance rate)

MECHANICS:
  - Quote-time checks decide whether to POST a resting quote (using a
    hypothetical "what if this and all resting quotes get accepted" worst case).
  - Confirm-time checks gate the ACTUAL fill (real committed payout + fill
    velocity), because quotes rest and acceptance timing is the customer's.
  - Near a cap, the quote WIDENS (via an inventory-skew term) until it declines,
    rather than hitting a hard cliff.
  - Everything fails closed; a "starvation watchdog" alarms if all flow is being
    silently blocked (blocking everything is itself a bug).

TWO CAVEATS BOTH DERIVATIONS INSISTED ON:
  1. CAPS LIMIT DOWNSIDE; THEY DO NOT CREATE PROFIT. Profit comes from the
     MARKUP. At thin markup EVERY cap set loses money in simulation (adverse
     selection); no cap fixes a book priced in the loss zone. These cap values
     assume a profitable markup is in force (above the break-even ~2c soccer /
     ~1c MLB). Caps + markup are a package.
  2. At $2,000 with these caps, probability of RUIN is ~0 across the whole range
     — the binding constraint is DRAWDOWN, not blow-up. Sized this way the book
     bleeds in a bad week (the halts catch it) but does not die.


================================================================================
9. THE FULL RFQ LIFECYCLE (how a combo flows through the system)
================================================================================
When a combo RFQ arrives:

 1. FILTERS         combos-only; sport allowlist (MLB+WC only); leg count; size;
                    not-halted. [decline -> stop]
 2. CLASSIFY        type every leg; UNKNOWN leg -> DECLINE (fail-closed);
                    detect logically-impossible or contained legs.
 3. PREGAME GATE    if ANY leg is in-play or its start-time is unknown -> DECLINE.
                    (Courtsider defense: never quote something a live in-venue
                    bettor could pick us off on.)
 4. PRICE           marginals from live leg books + correlation layer -> fair V.
 5. RISK CHECK      read the BOOK: what does THIS combo add? Check per-combo,
                    per-game, directional, absolute caps + fast Monte-Carlo
                    marginal tail impact. [breach -> decline]
 6. QUOTE BUILD     ask = fair + MARKUP + inventory SKEW (tighter if it balances
                    the book, wider if it concentrates); round in our favor;
                    near-cap -> widen into a decline.
 7. QUOTE OUT       post the NO-side quote (yes_bid=0), now resting.
 8. LAST LOOK       on customer accept, RE-CHECK: did a leg go in-play since we
                    quoted? fill-velocity / committed-payout budget ok? books
                    fresh? [fail -> lapse/decline]
 9. EXECUTE         fill booked; the BOOK + BALANCE update incrementally;
                    predicted vs exchange-ledger reconciled to the cent
                    (mismatch -> HALT).
10. SETTLE          parlay misses (NO) -> keep premium; hits (YES) -> pay $1/ct;
                    realized P&L to the ledger.

BACKGROUND LOOP (off the hot path, every maintenance tick): a full Monte-Carlo
over the WHOLE book -> P&L distribution, VaR/CVaR (tail loss), probability of a
large drawdown/ruin, per-game/leg tail contribution. Feeds the drawdown /
daily-loss / hard-trip halts (cancel-all + stop if breached) and refreshes the
inventory-skew targets used in step 6.


================================================================================
10. MONTE CARLO (designed; engine partly exists)
================================================================================
The bot already has a Monte-Carlo engine that simulates combo settlement under
the copula. The plan:
  - Simulate the ENTIRE book jointly (not one combo at a time) using the SAME
    correlation model the pricer uses (so risk and price are consistent). Legs
    within a game are correlated; different games are independent. This makes
    the GAME the risk unit in the simulation too.
  - Outputs: full P&L distribution; VaR and CVaR at 95%/99%; probability of
    ruin/large drawdown; per-game and per-leg contribution to tail loss; and the
    MARGINAL change in tail risk from accepting a candidate RFQ (this feeds the
    inventory skew — accept/price combos by whether they raise or lower
    portfolio tail risk).
  - Methodology emphasis: the dangerous number for a NO-seller is the small
    probability of a large correlated payout (ruin). Naive Monte Carlo estimates
    rare tails badly; the plan uses IMPORTANCE SAMPLING (Glasserman-Li style,
    treating games as common risk factors and parlay-hits as "defaults" — the
    portfolio-credit-risk analogy) to estimate the ruin probability precisely
    with far fewer paths. Plus variance reduction (control variate = the
    analytic fair) for the mean, and a settlement-graded VaR back-test to prove
    the tail estimate is not confidently wrong.

11. KEY EMPIRICAL FINDINGS THAT SHAPE THE RISK DESIGN
================================================================================
From a settlement-graded P&L study over one window (favorite-heavy; partially
settled — treated as directional, not precise):
  - Combos split into two tiers by how much markup the makers charge above our
    fair: "NORMAL" (tight, competitive) and "FAT" (makers charging a lot, mostly
    longshot corner/advance parlays).
  - ADVERSE-SELECTION ASYMMETRY (the durable finding): on NORMAL/competitive
    flow, widening our markup ATTRACTS the combos that hit (bad); on FAT/padded
    flow, widening SELECTS OUT the hitters (good). Same knob, opposite sign. So
    markup should be per-tier: thin/skip on competitive flow, fat on padded flow.
  - Both tiers resolve NO ~75% of the time (FAT is not "safer to sell" — it is
    just more overpriced by makers).
  - CONCENTRATION: a few games/combos dominate volume; correlated per-game
    payouts create ~$1M swings on a single game in-sample; hence the per-game
    caps and the whole game-keyed book.
  - CAPITAL: selling parlays ties up large gross exposure for thin premium;
    return-on-capital only becomes attractive on the fat tier at fat markup.


================================================================================
12. OPEN DECISIONS / KNOWN LIMITS (be skeptical here)
================================================================================
  - The cap %s above are START values from ONE window of data (soccer only 6
    games in the sample; MLB 53). They are to be run in SHADOW and re-derived
    from POOLED MULTI-WEEK, GAME-CLUSTERED settlement before gating real capital.
    We deliberately do NOT tune risk parameters on a single P&L window.
  - Classifying an incoming RFQ as NORMAL vs FAT at quote-time is unsolved: the
    "markup room" that defines the tier is only revealed AFTER the combo clears,
    so a live system needs a PREDICTOR of markup room from pre-quote features
    (leg spreads, family, favorite-skew, size). Planned as a shadow classifier.
  - The markup LEVELS themselves are deferred to multi-week data; the caps assume
    a profitable markup exists but do not set it.
  - Active hedging on single-leg markets is designed but off (would convert
    outright event risk into correlation risk); the sell-only mandate constrains
    only combo quotes, not leg markets.
  - Small-bankroll reality: at $2,000, position sizes are tiny slices of the
    available volume; the caps mostly govern how big a slice of each game we hold.


================================================================================
13. QUESTIONS WE MOST WANT A SECOND OPINION ON
================================================================================
  1. Is the TWO-AXIS accounting (true max-loss vs gross payout-obligation) the
     right decomposition for a sell-only NO book, and are we capping the right
     axis for each cap (loss caps on max-loss, utilization on gross)?
  2. Is the GAME the correct risk-aggregation unit, and are we missing a
     higher-order correlation (e.g. same-day, same-league, weather/injury
     regimes) that could correlate ACROSS games?
  3. Are the $2,000 cap levels sane? Too tight (no flow) or too loose (ruin)?
     Is drawdown-not-ruin the right thing to optimize at this size?
  4. Is importance sampling the right tool for the ruin estimate, and is the
     portfolio-credit-risk (games=factors, hits=defaults) analogy sound here?
  5. The adverse-selection asymmetry (markup attracts hitters on competitive
     flow, sheds them on padded flow) — is that mechanism real, and does the
     per-tier markup + room-predictor response make sense?
  6. Biggest blind spot: what failure mode are we NOT modeling? (Exchange
     outage mid-book, settlement disputes, a correlated-across-games shock,
     mass-acceptance faster than our cancel loop, a mispriced-marginal feed,
     adverse selection we can't see because the counterparty is informed.)
  7. For an autonomous bot on a small bankroll, is our halt/kill hierarchy
     (soft daily-loss -> drawdown -> hard-trip) sufficient, or do we need
     circuit breakers we haven't listed?
